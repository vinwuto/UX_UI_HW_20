Stonewall Columbus Redesign

Providing a vital LGBTQIA+ organization with a webpage fitting for their scope.

By Lily Homer, Cristin Kelly, Ian Richards, Vincent Wu

Research and Analysis

Stonewall Columbus is the main LGBTQIA+ center for the city of Columbus, Ohio.

Every year, they host the Columbus Pride parade, which welcomes over 700,000 participants annually for the festivities.

Given their scope and importance in the community, we felt the site did not fully encapsulate or give off the impression of an organization of their importance.


We first performed a heuristic evaluation of the site to identify the aspects of the site that worked, and the aspects of the site that we were not confident in.

Doing so allowed us to predict some of the problems that might come up during user testing, and to add them to our list.

While we waited to hear back from our shareholders at Stonewall Columbus, we scouted out participants for our interviews and user tests, and put out a survey on our social media.


Conducting our interviews and reading our survey answers yielded some valuable information that we could utilize in our redesign. In particular, some of the answers stuck out to us.

The Problem

Stonewall Columbus has an outdated website that is clunky and hard to navigate. How may we redesign the website to be more user-friendly, provide resources for Pride Parade attendees, and encapsulate the feeling of an LGBTQIA+ community center?

Ideation, Prototyping, and Iteration

In our user testing, we encountered some common issues that users had trouble with.

Aside from our anticipated issues with UI clarity, some of the problems we encountered included a lack of clarity when it came to site structure. With unclear buttons and a navbar that lacked consistency, we decided to re-map the site map to reduce confusion.

Our redesigned site map brought consistency back to the navigation, and we moved some pages around to better categories. In addition, we added an entire tab dedicated to Pride since it was one of their main focuses every year.


Our approach to prototyping was mobile-first to keep it clean and simple.

With our shareholder interview, we heard they were in the midst of their own redesign, so it was exciting to make a product that they potentially would reference.

Our prototype featured more pictures of the Columbus LGBTQIA+ community and a typeface designed by an LGBT typographer.

With our rough wireframe, we underwent user testing.

In our next few iterations, we added more color, gradients, cards, and finalized our style tile. After each iteration, we tested with users, which confirmed that our changes helped the site structure as well as the overall feel.

We then expanded our mobile prototype to create the website prototype. I personally took charge of this section and created microinteractions, such as the cards expanding on hover and the menu options flashing colors when hovered.

A challenging task at first, the redesign took off once we realized we had to be liberal with our use of color in order to capture the right style. All in all, a fun and unique project that helped me to understand microinteractions and the importance of catering to your users.
